HCI Design Sprint 3 - Fall 2025

Solar System Explorer

Kwasi Buansi, Tyler Thompson, Wendy Xu, Ivy Yuan - Davidson College

A VR game built in A-Frame that situates players in the solar system on a mission to find their boss's lost alien dog - without flying into the Sun or drifting out of range.

Introduction

The advent of virtual reality has opened numerous possibilities in the world of technology, enabling users to immerse themselves in new worlds. Our project situates players in the solar system, complete with all eight planets from Mercury to Neptune. The player's goal is to find their boss's lost alien dog without touching the Sun or leaving Earth's contact range. We built two versions - mobile using gaze controls, and PC using WASD - both demoed live to classmates using Google Cardboard VR headsets.

Ideating and Brainstorming

Before arriving at the solar system concept, we brainstormed a wide range of environments - deep-sea exploration, a serene campsite, fantasy landscapes. Outer space emerged as the unanimous direction. We initially imagined a passive flythrough, but decided a clear objective would make it more engaging. At our professor's recommendation, we centered gameplay around searching for a lost alien dog.

Technology and Development

We chose A-Frame over Unity for its accessibility - none of us had prior VR development experience, and A-Frame's web-based workflow allowed us to iterate quickly. Using A-Frame, we implemented a prototype solar system with all major celestial bodies positioned with real-life relative sizes and positions in mind.

Final Product

We broke development into three successive milestones.

Version 1.0 - Basic Solar System and Alien Dog

Established the base solar system and placed the alien dog as a static 2D image. ChatGPT-generated 3D model attempts were subpar, so we kept the flat image - which turned out to be charming.

Version 2.0 - Orbiting Planets and Vertical Movement

Enabled vertical movement (A-Frame defaults to x/z only), allowing players to view planets from above and below. Planets set to orbit the Sun at real-life proportional speeds, creating additional obstacles.

Version 3.0 - Background Music and Barking

Added atmospheric background music and a proximity-based barking system. Volume increases as you approach the dog and fades as you move away, significantly elevating the spatial experience.

Class Feedback

After demoing to classmates with Google Cardboard, we received two primary pieces of feedback.

Coordinate System Display

The x-y-z coordinate display was confusing out of everyday context. Proposed improvement: replace with a heliocentric (Sun-centered) coordinate display for more intuitive spatial orientation.

Alien Dog Dimensionality

The 2D alien dog felt visually detached from the 3D environment when viewed from above or below. Proposed improvement: develop a fully rigged 3D model to integrate naturally into the spatial environment.

Future Steps

  • 3D model for the alien dog, integrating naturally into the spatial environment
  • Behavioral animations so the dog wanders, encouraging active pursuit
  • Fatal meteors as a third failure condition, adding challenge
  • Reticle system for clearer targeting and catch mechanics
  • Feature parity between mobile and PC versions

Conclusion

Designing with VR technology allows imagination to expand in ways that traditional interfaces don't. We learned a great deal about version control, graphic design, and collaboration - and about the real constraints of cross-device development. Despite last-minute inconsistencies from hardware differences, the feedback we gathered gives us a clear path forward to a richer, more dynamic experience.
