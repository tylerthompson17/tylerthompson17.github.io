Visualizing Flight Etiquette

Introduction

In an increasingly data-driven world, it's more crucial than ever for individuals to be able to comprehend datasets. Data visualizations serve different purposes - analytical visualizations aim to provide clear and unbiased insights, while persuasive visualizations are designed to resonate with audiences and weave a compelling narrative.

The Data

We worked with a FiveThirtyEight flight etiquette dataset covering respondents' views on in-flight behaviors - armrest use, reclining seats, bringing kids on flights - alongside demographic data including age, sex, income, education, and census region. The data was cleaned by removing any responses with blanks, and future-looking features were dropped to prevent data leakage.

Analytical Visualizations

We used the Five Design Sheets (FdS) methodology to ideate and refine our analytical designs across five sheets.

Design Sheet #1: Ideation

We sketched bar charts, scatterplots, cartograms, and parallel coordinates to brainstorm the best way to depict demographics, preferences, and perceptions of rudeness. Three key ideas emerged: color-coded bar charts, parallel coordinates for demographics, and demographic filters for interactivity.

Design Sheet #2: Parallel Coordinates

Task: Overview of respondents' demographics. Each parallel axis corresponds to a demographic type - gender, height, education. Line thickness indicates the number of respondents matching that profile, making trends immediately visible.

Design Sheet #3: Bar Charts & Gauges

Task: Compare rudeness response types by question. Color-coded bars with demographic filters, complemented by a gauge visualization that gives an immediate read on how rude a given action is.

Design Sheet #4: Pie Charts

Task: Highlight respondents' preferences. Pie charts were chosen over bar charts because preference questions had more potential answers. Segments are labeled in percentages with a collapsible legend and demographic filters.

Design Sheet #5: Final Integration

Finalized designs stayed largely consistent with sheets two through four. The key change was replacing a single demographic dropdown with multiple filters at the top of each page, allowing viewers to control for multiple demographics simultaneously.

Analysis Visualization #1 - Parallel Coordinates

Each axis maps a demographic category. Line thickness encodes respondent count per demographic profile, making trends immediately visible across the full dataset.

Feedback & Reflection

Future improvements include adding tooltips, ensuring consistent subset ordering across axes, and increasing interactivity for viewers.

Analysis Visualization #2 - Bar Charts of Perceived Rudeness

Color-coded response breakdowns for each rudeness question, with multiple demographic filters that can be applied simultaneously for granular cross-demographic comparison.

Feedback & Reflection

Classmates flagged that expanding a bar chart distorted the message - bar area rather than length became the visual signal. Future fix: reduce expanded bar height to maintain length as the primary encoding.

Analysis Visualization #3 - Rudeness Gauges

Each gauge maps an average rudeness score to three ranges: not rude (0–0.33), somewhat rude (0.33–0.66), and very rude (0.66–1). The indicator and arc color communicate the range at a glance.

Analysis Visualization #4 - Pie Charts of Preferences

Preference questions displayed as filterable pie charts. Chosen over bar charts given the higher number of response options per question.

Persuasive Visualizations

The persuasive suite argues a single thesis: rudeness is not a fixed standard but a culturally negotiated concept shaped by regional norms. Three visualizations tell the story in sequence.

Design Sheet #1: Ideation

Purely exploratory - heat maps, bar charts, scatter plots, and interactive dashboards were all considered. Three directions stood out: a choropleth map, a diverging bar chart, and a scatter plot.

Design Sheet #2: Choropleth Map

A US map where color intensity reflects average perceived rudeness per region. Static global color bins ensure fair cross-behavior comparison - the entry point for the persuasive argument.

Design Sheet #3: Diverging Bar Chart

Bars split around a neutral midpoint showing % "not rude" left and % "rude" right per region. Colorblind-safe palette, normalized percentages, and white bar labels for readability.

Design Sheet #4: Scatter Plot

Each region plotted by overall rudeness score vs. share of "very rude" responses. Dot size encodes respondent count. Quadrant labels (Easily Offended, Indifferent, etc.) make the argument explicit.

Design Sheet #5: Final Integration

Each visualization occupies its own page, guiding the audience through a sequential narrative: where differences occur → how strong they are → why they exist.

Persuasion Visualization #1 - Regional Rudeness Map

Color intensity reflects average perceived rudeness per census region. Dropdown selects the behavior; hover reveals state-level detail. Darker = higher sensitivity to rudeness.

Changes from Sketches

Switched from per-question tertile bins to global static bins so comparisons across behaviors are fair. Darkened the lightest color for accessibility.

Persuasion Visualization #2 - Diverging Bar Chart

Bars split at a neutral midpoint - % "not rude" left, % "rude" right - making polarization immediately readable across regions. Some actions show near-consensus; others reveal sharp cultural divides.

Persuasion Visualization #3 - Regional Scatter Plot

Regions plotted by overall rudeness score vs. share of "very rude" responses. Quadrant labels make the conclusion explicit: regional patterns are consistent and culturally meaningful, not random.

Demo Video

The video demonstrates how persuasive and analytical visualizations serve different purposes - analytical views invite free exploration, while persuasive views guide interpretation toward a specific conclusion.

Key Insight

Politeness standards aren't universal - they're shaped by regional expectations and shared social values. By structuring the persuasive visualizations as a sequential narrative (where, how strong, why), viewers are guided to a conclusion rather than left to find it themselves.
